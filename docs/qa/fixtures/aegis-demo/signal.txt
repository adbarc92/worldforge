THE HALION ANOMALY — OBSERVATIONAL NOTES

The anomaly was first recorded in 2401 by automated probes dropped into the upper atmosphere of Halion. The pulse emerges from a region approximately 40,000 kilometers below the cloud deck and propagates outward in a roughly spherical wavefront. Duration is consistently 3.7 seconds. Amplitude varies by a factor of ten between events. The period between events is 47 hours plus or minus four minutes.

The pulse occupies a narrow band near 1420 MHz — the so-called hydrogen line — which has led Dr. Rho and others to argue for an intentional origin. Her 2410 paper proposes that the waveform exhibits non-random structure when sampled at 12-bit resolution, though this claim remains controversial. Dr. Fen's analysis, published six months later, found no statistically significant structure at any sampling resolution tested.

The anomaly does not appear to degrade the station's systems directly, but transient EM effects have been observed during every pulse: elevator doors cycling unprompted, medical monitors briefly showing flatline, the central computing core dropping a handful of non-critical processes. These effects last only the duration of the pulse itself.

No other celestial body in the Kepler-186 system emits comparable signals. Halion's three moons are silent. The star is quiet. Whatever produces the anomaly, if it is not the planet itself, originates from inside Halion.

The Commonwealth Defense classification on the anomaly data was extended in 2411 for "further review." No timeline has been given for its release.
